442A Ironworker Salary in Canada (2026)

Wages by province, experience level, union status, and Red Seal premium

The Ironworker trade consistently ranks among the highest-paid construction trades in Canada. Structural steel erection, reinforcing rod, and ornamental work all command premium wages — particularly for union members on large industrial and infrastructure projects. Ironworker Wages by Province (2026)

Union wages for Ironworkers are negotiated through collective agreements between the Iron Workers union locals and employer associations. The following rates reflect current journeyperson base wages — total compensation including benefits, pension contributions, and vacation pay can add 25–35% to these figures.

Alberta

$50–$58/hr

Alberta consistently offers the highest Ironworker wages in Canada, driven by oil sands industrial construction, pipeline projects, and Calgary/Edmonton urban development. Union journeypersons on major industrial sites regularly earn $55+ per hour, with premium pay for remote locations and shift work adding further.

British Columbia

$47–$54/hr

BC Ironworkers benefit from major LNG facility construction, Site C dam work, and Vancouver area high-rise projects. The Lower Mainland is one of the most active construction markets in North America, keeping wages competitive with Alberta for most project types.

Ontario

$44–$52/hr

Ontario has the largest volume of Ironworker employment in Canada. Toronto's construction boom has sustained demand for structural steel erectors, while major infrastructure work (bridges, transit, highways) provides stable employment. Union rates in the GTA are among the highest in the country.

Saskatchewan

$42–$49/hr

Saskatchewan wages are strong for journeypersons working in potash mining projects and infrastructure. Smaller overall market than Alberta but fewer ironworkers competing for positions.

Manitoba

$39–$46/hr

Manitoba wages are slightly below the western average. Major hydro projects (Manitoba Hydro expansion) periodically spike demand and wages. Winnipeg's construction market provides steady baseline employment.

Quebec

$40–$47/hr

Quebec Ironworkers are governed by the Commission de la construction du Québec (CCQ) rather than the Iron Workers union, with distinct collective agreements. Bridge and infrastructure renewal projects have driven significant demand over the past decade.

Atlantic Provinces (NB, NS, PEI, NL)

$34–$42/hr

Atlantic wages are lower than the national average, reflecting smaller markets and lower cost of living. Newfoundland is an exception during major offshore oil and gas construction cycles, where rates can approach Alberta levels.

Red Seal Premium

Many union collective agreements for Ironworkers include an explicit Red Seal wage premium — typically $1.00 to $3.00 per hour above the standard journeyperson rate. Over a full year of work (2,000 hours), this adds $2,000–$6,000 in annual earnings. Over a 25-year career, the compounding effect — including pension contributions calculated on higher wages — can exceed $200,000 in additional lifetime earnings.

Even in agreements where there is no explicit Red Seal premium, employers making hiring decisions between two equally experienced candidates will generally favour the Red Seal holder, particularly for supervisory roles where the premium can exceed $5/hour.

Overtime, Shift Premiums, and Remote Pay

Total annual earnings for Ironworkers often significantly exceed what base hourly rates suggest, because of several premium pay categories:

  • Overtime: 1.5× after 8 hours/day or 40 hours/week; some agreements pay 2× after 10 hours. Structural steel erection projects frequently run extended shifts.
  • Shift differentials: Afternoon and night shifts typically attract 10–15% premiums
  • Remote camp allowances: Projects in remote locations (oil sands, mining, hydro) pay room-and-board or daily living allowances of $80–$200/day in addition to wages
  • Travel allowances: Union agreements often include travel time pay and mileage for projects beyond a certain radius from the union hall
Top-earner scenario: An Alberta Ironworker with Red Seal, working a remote industrial project at $58/hr base + $2/hr Red Seal premium + $150/day camp allowance, working 10 hours/day for 200 days = approximately $160,000–$170,000 in total annual compensation. This is not unusual during major project cycles.

Annual Earnings Outlook

Estimating annual earnings requires accounting for typical employment patterns — construction is seasonal in most of Canada, and even the busiest markets have slow periods. Union Ironworkers in strong markets average 1,600–2,000 paid hours per year. In remote project work, 2,200–2,400 hours in a single year is achievable.

  • Apprentice Year 1 (Ontario): approximately $45,000–$55,000
  • Journeyperson (Ontario, union): approximately $80,000–$100,000
  • Journeyperson (Alberta, union): approximately $95,000–$120,000
  • Journeyperson + Red Seal + industrial remote (Alberta): $130,000–$170,000
  • Foreman/General Foreman: Add 15–30% above journeyperson rate
