Province-by-province wage data for 421A Red Seal technicians — from apprentice to journeyperson and beyond.

One of the most common questions from people considering the Heavy Equipment Technician trade is straightforward: how much does it actually pay? The honest answer is that it varies considerably — by province, by sector, and by how much you're willing to go where the work is. This guide breaks it down as accurately as possible based on current market data.

Where the Real Money Is — Sector Breakdown

Oil Sands (Fort McMurray, AB)

The oil sands remain the highest-paying environment for HETs in Canada. Technicians working on Komatsu 930E haul trucks, Caterpillar 793s, and draglines routinely earn $55–$80/hour. With camp rotations (2 weeks in / 1 week out is common), annual take-home regularly reaches $130,000–$180,000 including overtime.

Underground Mining

Underground mines across BC, Ontario, Quebec, and New Brunswick pay a premium for technicians willing to work underground. The environment is more demanding, and wages reflect that — $50–$70/hour is common. Remote northern mines often add northern allowances on top.

Equipment Dealers (Finning, Toromont, Brandt)

Dealerships are often where apprentices get their start. Journeyperson wages at major Caterpillar and John Deere dealers typically run $40–$55/hour with good benefits packages, pension plans, and company vehicles for field service technicians. The work-life balance is generally better than site work.

Municipal/Government

City and regional district shops maintain road graders, loaders, and snow removal equipment. Pay is typically $38–$50/hour with excellent benefits, defined-benefit pension plans, and stable hours. Overtime is limited but the job security is strong.

Field Service vs. Shop — What's the Difference in Pay?

Field service technicians — who travel to equipment breakdowns on-site — typically earn a 10–20% premium over shop rates. They also usually receive a company truck, fuel card, and tool allowance. The tradeoff is irregular hours and travel.

Real talk on overtime: In construction and mining, 50–60 hour weeks are common during busy seasons. At $55/hour straight time, overtime at 1.5x works out to $82.50/hour. This is how annual incomes in the trades can significantly exceed what the hourly rate suggests.

Does the Red Seal Actually Increase Your Pay?

Yes — and in two ways. First, most employers pay journeyperson rates only upon certification, which is typically a jump of $4–$8/hour over your 4th year apprentice rate. Second, the Red Seal opens doors to employers in other provinces and to more specialized roles that require certified status.

Some provinces (notably Alberta) also have Red Seal bonus programs that pay technicians a one-time incentive for obtaining their certification — check your provincial apprenticeship authority for current programs.

Benefits and Total Compensation

Beyond hourly wages, most unionized and many non-union HET positions include:

  • Health and dental benefits (individual and family coverage)
  • Pension or RRSP matching
  • Tool allowance ($500–$2,000/year is common)
  • Safety boot and PPE allowance
  • Company vehicle or vehicle allowance for field techs
  • Camp accommodation, meals, and travel for remote sites

Career Progression and Advancement

The HET trade doesn't have to be a ceiling. Common advancement paths include:

  • Lead Technician / Shop Foreman — supervise other techs, $60–$80/hour equivalent
  • Service Manager — move into management, salary-based
  • Technical Trainer / Field Support Rep — work for OEMs (Caterpillar, Komatsu, Deere) training other techs
  • Parts and Service Sales — leverage technical knowledge in a sales role
  • Business Owner — independent repair shop or mobile service

Job Outlook — Is HET a Good Trade to Enter in 2026?

Demand for Heavy Equipment Technicians is strong and forecast to remain so through the end of the decade. Several converging factors are driving this:

  • Aging workforce. A significant share of the current HET workforce is within 10 years of retirement. Provinces like Alberta and BC are actively running shortage programs to attract new apprentices into the trade.
  • Infrastructure spending. Federal and provincial infrastructure programs — roads, bridges, transit corridors, LNG facilities — are keeping construction equipment fleets running at full capacity, which means more hours for technicians.
  • Mining expansion. Potash, copper, and critical mineral mining projects in Saskatchewan, BC, and Ontario have added thousands of pieces of heavy equipment to the national fleet over the past five years.
  • Fleet electrification transition. OEMs like Caterpillar, Komatsu, and Volvo are introducing battery-electric and hydrogen fuel cell variants of haul trucks and excavators. Technicians with EV diagnostic skills will command even higher wages as this transition accelerates through 2026–2030.

According to the Government of Canada's Job Bank, employment prospects for Heavy Duty Equipment Technicians are rated "Good" in most provinces, with Alberta, BC, and Saskatchewan showing the tightest labour markets.

Camp Pay and Remote Site Premiums

One of the most significant — and often misunderstood — parts of HET compensation is remote site pay. When you work a fly-in/fly-out rotation at an oil sands or mining operation, your effective hourly rate is much higher than what your pay stub shows:

  • Camp accommodation and meals are employer-provided — no rent, no groceries during your work rotation. This alone is worth $150–$250/day in living expenses.
  • Northern allowances are common in remote locations — an extra $2–$5/hour on top of your journeyperson rate.
  • Overtime is almost guaranteed. Most camp rotations run 10–12 hour days, 7 days a week. At 1.5x overtime, a $50/hour base rate becomes $75/hour for every hour over 8 per day.
  • Travel pay. Most remote employers pay 8 hours of travel time each way on your travel days — you're paid to fly to and from camp.

A journeyperson earning a base rate of $52/hour on a typical 2-week-on/1-week-off rotation, accounting for all overtime and savings on living expenses, can accumulate the equivalent of $180,000–$200,000 in annual compensation. This is why so many HETs in their late 20s and early 30s are mortgage-free.

Tips for Maximizing Your HET Income

Beyond just finding a job, there are specific strategies that experienced technicians use to reach the top of the wage range faster:

  • Get your Red Seal as fast as possible. Every month you spend at apprentice rates instead of journeyperson rates is income left on the table. Study consistently throughout your apprenticeship, not just the month before your exam.
  • Target the equipment you want to work on. Technicians specializing in high-value equipment — large mining haul trucks, hydraulic excavators over 100 tonnes, blasthole drills — command higher wages than generalist light-equipment technicians.
  • Build OEM-specific certifications. Caterpillar, Komatsu, Liebherr, and Volvo CE all offer certified technician programs. These certifications are required for warranty work and make you more valuable to dealers and large fleet operators.
  • Consider a fly-in/fly-out rotation early in your career. Even one or two years at a remote site builds savings and resume depth that translates into higher wages at your next employer.
  • Track your specializations. If you've completed high-voltage training (1000V+), confined space rescue, or thermal imaging certification, document these — they directly affect what rate you can negotiate.
